RIVERSIDE POLICE DEPARTMENT
SUSPECT INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT
Interview No.: INT-2024-0317-003
Date: Sunday, March 17, 2024 — Interview commenced 09:30 AM
Subject: LYDIA FERRO, age 38
Address: 91 Harlow Gardens, Riverside
Occupation: Head of Conservation, Riverside Museum of Art and Antiquities (5 years)
Interviewing Officers: Detective Louis Harte, Detective Sergeant Priya Mehta
Subject's solicitor: Not requested.
Interview location: Interview Room 1, Riverside Police Department

NOTE: Ms. Ferro is interviewed as a person of interest given her professional
proximity to both the victim and the museum. She was not on premises at the
time of the incident but is included in the active witness/suspect pool
pending investigation of museum personnel relationships.

[RECORDING BEGINS]

DET. HARTE: Ms. Ferro, thank you for coming in. You understand this is
voluntary — you're not under arrest, you're free to leave.

LYDIA FERRO: I understand. Whatever I can do to help. Eleanor was — she was
a mentor to me. I'm still in shock.

DET. MEHTA: We're sorry for your loss. Let's start with Friday. What time
did you leave the museum?

FERRO: I left around 5:45. Maybe five to six. I had a dinner reservation.

DET. HARTE: Where was dinner?

FERRO: Marechal's. The French restaurant on Cavendish Street. I'd made a
reservation months ago — a friend was in town. Dr. Christine Okafor — she's
a conservation specialist from the British Museum. We had a 7:00 PM reservation.

DET. MEHTA: And Dr. Okafor can confirm that?

FERRO: She's already contacted your department, I'm told. Yes. We were at
Marechal's from around 7:00 PM to almost 10:00 PM. Three hours. The restaurant
has our booking. There were four of us.

DET. HARTE: So you were at the restaurant from 7:00 PM to approximately 10:00 PM?

FERRO: Yes. We have the receipt. I paid on my card — it will show the time.
The restaurant closes at eleven, we were there until nearly ten.

DET. MEHTA: What was your relationship with Eleanor Voss like?

FERRO: Professional. Respectful. She was Head Curator, I run conservation.
We worked together closely — approval processes for treatments, loan conditions,
acquisition condition reports. We didn't always agree but I respected her
enormously.

DET. HARTE: Did you have any conflicts with her recently?

FERRO: No. There was a minor disagreement in February about the Hargrove
items — I had concerns about the storage conditions for two pieces, and
Eleanor wanted to rush them into a display. We resolved it. It was a
professional discussion, not a conflict.

DET. HARTE: Did Eleanor mention anything to you about concerns she had with
the acquisitions process or with anyone at the museum?

FERRO: [pause] She mentioned — vaguely — that she was reviewing some of the
recent acquisition costs. About two weeks ago. She said she'd found something
that "didn't add up" and she was being careful about how to handle it. She
didn't tell me what or who specifically. I had the impression it was sensitive.
I didn't push her.

DET. MEHTA: Did she mention Marcus Bellweather?

FERRO: Not directly. But the acquisitions department is Marcus's domain. It
wasn't hard to infer.

DET. HARTE: And you never raised that inference with anyone — with HR,
with the Board?

FERRO: Eleanor asked me not to say anything yet. She said she needed to be
certain before she said anything to anyone. I respected that. I'm — I wish
I'd pushed harder now.

DET. MEHTA: Is there anything else you want to tell us?

FERRO: Just that whoever did this to Eleanor — it wasn't a random act.
Eleanor was careful, intelligent, methodical. If she was killed, it's because
she knew something and someone didn't want her to reveal it.

[RECORDING ENDS — 10:44 AM]
