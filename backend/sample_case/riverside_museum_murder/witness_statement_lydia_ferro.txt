STATEMENT OF LYDIA FERRO

I understand why you want to speak to me. Eleanor and I were colleagues for eight years. We had a — complicated professional relationship. And yes, we argued. I won't pretend otherwise, because I know people will have told you about it.

The argument on Wednesday the 13th — it was about exhibit attribution. Eleanor had approved copy for the upcoming Victorian Decorative Arts catalogue that listed the curatorial lead as "museum staff" for the Hartley Collection section. I had done the primary research on that collection for fourteen months before I left the museum. That work is mine. Eleanor knew that and approved catalogue language that erased my contribution entirely. I confronted her about it. It was heated. I raised my voice. I regret the venue — it was in the main atrium, staff and a few late visitors heard it. But I said nothing threatening. I told her it was a professional disgrace and I intended to write to the board. That's all.

I did not return to the museum on Friday evening. I have no reason to. I haven't had regular access to the building since I resigned in 2022.

On Friday evening, March 15th, I was at dinner at Cala Bistro, 18 Promenade Walk. I arrived at 6:50 PM. I was with two colleagues — Dr. Harriet Omolade and Professor James Fitch, both from Riverside University. We had a table from approximately 7:00 PM until approximately 9:15 PM. Our bill was settled at 9:12 PM — you can verify this with the restaurant.

I also received a phone call at 8:22 PM during dinner from my department administrator, Cynthia Barret, regarding a Monday morning scheduling conflict. The call lasted approximately eight minutes. My phone records will confirm this. I excused myself from the table to take the call in the restaurant's entrance area.

Dr. Omolade and Professor Fitch can confirm all of this.

I did not kill Eleanor Voss. Regardless of what happened between us professionally, I had no reason to harm her and certainly no intention of doing so. If I was angry enough to put something in writing to the board, I had no need to do anything else. I was angry about my work being erased. That is different from what you're suggesting.

To my knowledge, Eleanor had been having some kind of dispute within the museum — not with me, but internally. I had heard from a mutual contact, Dr. Fitch actually, that Eleanor had grown concerned about some irregularities in the museum's acquisitions records. She didn't share the details with me — we were not on speaking terms beyond the necessary — but she mentioned to James in passing, at a conference reception in February, that she had "started pulling threads she wished she hadn't." I don't know what that means exactly. You might want to look into that.

I have nothing further to add.
